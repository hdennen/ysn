Position Specific Coaching Sets Investec Rugby Academy Apart

By: 
Sazi Luke
Lungelo Mdladla
Tue, 18/12/2012 - 00:53

"I knew at that stage we had to develop the highest level of rugby development programs in the world, and to do that you had to get the best people." -Murray Mexted

Since its inception twelve years ago, the Investec International Rugby Academy in New Zealand has grown from strength to strength in advancing junior and senior rugby players and coaches. Four years ago the academy was brought to South Africa headed by Ruan Fourie and Dick Muir with the purpose of emulating the same level of training and skills coaching.

This year the Academy was held at the University of KwaZulu-Natal from the 9th to the 15th of December. Players and coaches from around the country were taught skills and techniques from some of the world’s best former rugby players and specialist coaches. Players from EG Jansen, Affies, Michaelhouse, St. John’s, and Grey Bloem to name a few were all present to learn and absorb the information and lessons taught by the academy coaches and specialists. 

On Friday the teams played a match split into four quarters, and in between each quarter the academy coaches came onto the field to coach the players in their specific regions. The academy stresses the value of role position coaching as the practice allows players to be taught specifically on their position thus improving their specialised skill.

HSSM’s Sazi Luke chatted to a few former international rugby players and specialists to learn a bit more on the Investec International Rugby Academy

Ruan Fourie, Co-founder of the SA Franchise

HSSM: Ruan, can you tell me a bit more about the academy and how long it has been running for?
Ruan Fourie: The academy has been running for four years […] in which I would say we have had about 380 boys through the academy. We are not a mainstream academy like the Sharks Academy; we host position specific coaching, courses for players and coaches. We coach the coaches how to coach the players so that makes us different from other academies. And we only take two players per position so those few players get to be coached by an Ex-international in their own position so they understand the role a bit better. For instance last year 2011 we had five players, those five players that have come through the academy play in the junior world cup, three for South Africa, one for Australia and one for Scotland. So it’s about really specific, one on one coaching for anyone that wants to make rugby a career.

HSSM: What would you say is the objective of starting and running an academy such as this one?
Ruan Fourie: Our objective is […] to take anyone to the next level. Let’s say you play 2nd’s and you want to play for the 1st’s, [...] we give them specialist coaching and my aim is to deliver springboks, and the reason for that is we are the only academy in the world that really does position specific coaching. You will see 25 Ex-internationals here coaching two guys in their position. Players walk away here understanding their role. Let’s say you’re Dick Muir, he can’t show a hooker how to hook, he has never been a hooker and that’s why we are position specific. […] We have John Allen here, […] he played for South Africa and played for Scotland and he handles the hooking. We’ve got Braam van Straaten that works with the fly halves and he is the current Australian kicking coach. And that’s what it really is, it’s about specific coaching in your position so you can understand your role and be a better player.

HSSM: What notable players have come through the ranks in the past few years?
Ruan Fourie: We have got so many success stories, as I said we have delivered 5 players in the last junior world cup. Our sister academy in New Zealand […] which has been running for 12 years, Players like Craig Burden when he needed to switch from positions went there. […] Currently in South Africa, Patrick Howard for the Stormers in the super rugby next year, Tony Janties who’s playing at the Bulls and Braam Steyn that’s playing 8th man at the Sharks. We got Tommy Allen that played for Scotland this year, so ja, we have had a couple of guys coming through the ranks and we have numerous Craven Week players, too many to mention, and in a few years we shall see them at senior level.

WP Eloff, Affies 1st team player

HSSM: WP, can you tell me about how you got involved in this academy as a player?
WP Eloff: This is my 2nd Investec academy. I first came here as a lock and this year I moved to prop. Now I’m learning to tight head and this tour just opened up everything for me and that’s why I have spent all this money because it’s more than worth it.

HSSM: What do you think an academy such as this one does for young kids aspiring to be professionals such as yourself?
WP Eloff: It’s just seven days, and after the sixth day you play a game and must gel with other guys that you never knew and play against a team that has been a team for a year or two. So like just push through and all those ball runs and everything just plants something in your heart […] getting bigger and more involved in everything.

HSSM: So do you feel it would help you with the transition to playing professional?
WP Eloff: Definitely!

SA franchise co-founder and former Sharks Coach, Dick Muir, along with former Natal and Springbok player, James Small

HSSM: Dick Muir and James Small, can you tell me a bit more about your involvement in the Academy?
Dick Muir: I’ve been involved from the start which was launched four and a half years ago. […] I went over in 2007 to the international rugby academy in New Zealand and met up with Murray [Mexted] and was really amazed at the amount of information sharing that went on at a top level. I was coaching the Sharks at the time, and when I said to Murray this was badly needed in South Africa, he said that Nick Mallet and many other coaches that have been on the course had said exactly the same. So we embarked on a campaign to create a franchise business and started this, and with the support of Investec it’s just gone from strength to strength.
James Small: Dickies [Dick Muir] calls in the experts. [laughs] No but it’s about sharing knowledge hey, just like Dick said it’s about taking the experience that we have as players and trying to give the guys an advantage because if they listen a little bit… I mean we have got a guy like David Campese here today who has played over a 100 test matches. So there is a lot of experience here that a guy can take advantage of.

HSSM: What type of impact will an academy such as this one have on kids in the long run, or even South African rugby as a whole?
Dick Muir: Well if I could give you an example, we have had a few successes. […] If you take a guy like Francious Steyn, he went on the New Zealand course and I was coaching the Sharks at the time and it was through cross reference with Murray [Mexted] that he got his opportunity to play for the Sharks, and two weeks later he basically became a Springbok. For me sometimes when you work with players and have access to ex-internationals who are specialists in their own their  position and they work with a player and they identify that talent, you can take it that it’s pretty accurate that they know whether that guy can kick on or not.
James Small: I think it being only four years old, most of the kids that we are with are only 16, 17 years old. So I think the development of players and the fact that they have been to this academy will come out in the future when they actually get to playing for provincial sides and super 15 sides. But you will see players coming through.
Dick Muir: Just to add onto that, the amount of influence that the coach has on a number of kids that he coaches is huge, and when they have been exposed to this sort of information coming out of a course like this it fast-tracks their progress.

David Campese, Former Australian Rugby Player with 101 caps for the Wallabies

HSSM: David, can you tell us about your involvement in the academy?
David Campese: I had moved out of Australia in December last year and obviously I like coaching and tried getting involved. So I telephoned Dick [Muir] and this has been the 3rd or 4th course I have been on.

HSSM: How do you feel a course like this helps these young kids who are aspiring to become rugby stars?
David Campese: I just think it gives them an opportunity to listen and learn from ex-players who have been there done that. Because sometimes when you grow the coaches are very enthusiastic, but the knowledge is not probably there. […] It gives them an understanding if they will want to go on and be a rugby professional we can help in any way. What we say and our actions will help them down the track.

HSSM: What do you think having big names such as yourself do to an academy such as this one?
David Campese: Firstly I think it helps with Investec because they are a very big name as well, so having available big names makes it appealing to most people, and the coaches as well because the coaches that have come through learn a lot. […] There was a coach who said he has been to many clinics and said he has learnt more in five days than any other place. What we do is actually challenge the players and the coaches. We don’t say you do this, we say you’ve got to try decide which way you want to go – is this good is this bad. What you do is actually empower the player and the coach to actually make decisions.

HSSM: What type of coach’s skills and qualities are you planning on improving?
David Campese: What we try to and what is one of my pet peeves are basic skills, doing the very simple things properly. For me that is very important because if you can’t catch, pass, support, run straight, and communicate – and that’s what rugby is – if you can’t do those simple things it’s very hard to do the level up.

Murray Mexted former New Zealand 8th man and founder of the Academy

HSSM: Mr Mexted can you tell us about the history of the academy and how the idea came about as a founder?
Murray Mexted: Sure, I think when the game looked like it was going to be professional it became very obvious to me that as a small country with a small number of players – this is in New Zealand in a small economy – we would lose all our player to professional teams around the world and we would become a very weak All Blacks side. So I knew at that stage we had to develop the highest level of rugby development programs in the world, and to do that you had to get the best people. So I sat down with a mate of mine and drew up a plan of how it could come about and went to the New Zealand Rugby Union, […] formed a pretty strong board though, my board included a chairman of the IRB and New Zealand Rugby Union […] so it was a pretty powerful board. In effect we got an alliance agreement with NZRU to go ahead and develop the best high performance academy in the world. Since then we have targeted players in New Zealand that academies have got and want to develop into stars and guys who haven’t quite made regional academies. Now about a third of all New Zealand players are our graduates and that’s in a space of 12 years.   

HSSM: “How has the academy grown in those 12 years and which notable players have come through?”
Murray Mexted: “Israel Dagg, Aaron Cruden, Cory Jane, Julian Savea, and Charlie Faumuina and those are all the new young starts of the All Black team. “

HSSM: “How do you think this New Zealand concept has translated into the South African rugby system?”
Murray Mexted: “It’s been very good. It’s a different market we have found because you have got to have a lot more players, but it’s development here so it’s a bigger job. And it has been hard work but it’s gaining momentum really nicely. It’s very pleasing, […] you look in five years’ time the states will be similar, these guys will be punching out a lot of players playing in your super rugby in the country. They are already doing that actually and getting achievement from the young guys.”
