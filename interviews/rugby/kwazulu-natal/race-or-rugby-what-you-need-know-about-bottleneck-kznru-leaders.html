Race or Rugby? What You Need to Know About The Bottleneck - KZNRU Leaders Interview Part 3

By: 
Sazi Luke
Harry Dennen
Wed, 07/11/2012 - 14:01

“Why have we not developed the game of rugby quicker than we have?” – Pete Smith, KZNRU CEO

In part three of this four part KZNRU Leaders Interview, HSSM’s Sazi Luke gets Pete Smith and Graham Mackenzie to give their take on the hard topics of race, quotas, culture, and what ‘the bottleneck’ really means.

HSSM: Is there still that looming thought that people of colour that come into the rugby system, that there’s a quota system involved? Is that still the case? in this day are there still quotas and that?
Smith: No, that’s dissipated a lot in five years in my opinion.
MacKenzie: Let’s just look at it, at school level there are no quotas and I think there’s a natural integration of players of all colours, which is fantastic. In fact some of the schools, the old second tier, third tier schools in many cases are 90% black today. I mean if you look at George Campbell for example, which just ten years ago was considered an Afrikaner white environment sort of school. Today if you look at their make-up of their firsts, seconds, and thirds, it’s completely black. So there’s been a natural transformation that’s gone on. And that’s what we seek in our country. So that’s the first thing, but the other thing, at club rugby I can tell you now in the premier league, and I’ll spring to the Rovers guys today who are playing in the quarter finals of the SARU National Club Championships, the main starting line-up today in the quarter finals, I think eight of the starting fifteen are players of colour, up in Rustenburg, and that’s merit.
Smith: Because it’s got like that, the thought of quotas is sort of long ago. If Rovers were running out with two players there today, you know, the people sitting watching there at the club champs taking place this week, might think: ‘I wonder if those two players are, I see it’s a wing and a prop, I bet you those are quotas. Are there quotas at this tournament?’ That’s how thinking has gone in terms of school and club rugby, it’s filtered to the provincial level as well. We’re leaving that behind, thank goodness.

“they have put in quotas in rural areas where there are very few players, black or white, coming through”

MacKenzie: Let me just tell you the new national sports plan speaks to the issue of quotas again. So let me just come and talk about it because it’s a ticklish area. We were discussing with netball last week in Cape Town how they were going to handle it. For example, they put in quotas not in areas where there’s a high density of players, but they have put in quotas in rural areas where there are very few players, black or white, coming through. For example in Zululand, they’ve got a problem because all the netball girls there are black. They can’t find any white girls, so they’ve got a reverse problem. Whereas in Johannesburg central they’ve got a lot of white girls and not enough black youngsters, so they’ve taken a very pragmatic approach to say, ‘we’re not going to penalize you, but Zululand try and find one or two white girls, even if they’re on the bench. So I think the thought of quotas is that, although the new sports plan talks to it, how that will be implemented and at what level I’m not sure. My sense is that it probably will be implemented at a level to encourage more youngsters coming through, probably at a club level and lower down where there is clearly no development of any description. I think that’s what they’re talking about here.
HSSM: So do you feel that development in rugby has penetrated into the community?
MacKenzie: Not nearly enough. We’re the first to admit that. I mean if there’s an African family in Mlazi that wakes up on a Saturday morning, do they sit around a coffee table and say, geez we’re going to watch rugby this afternoon? That’s the mentality we want to get to. I think you’ll agree as rugby people. And it might be with a few families, like our rugby community, but to make it bigger than that we’ve got some work to do to get to that.
HSSM: How much do the cultural barriers play into this because South Africa has a past, and that past has been associated with rugby? Are there cultural challenges that you guys are facing? Are you decolourizing rugby to not make it about colour? Is that a challenge that you guys have met while trying to take rugby to these communities?

“There’s always going to be the cultural challenge, at the end of the day we want as many of our youngsters playing rugby.”

MacKenzie: Well I think we’re meeting it. Traditionally in a KZNRU perspective we have a massive challenge, like Mpumalanga would have a massive challenge and some other regions in our country. But I can tell you that if you go to the Eastern Cape, if you go to Border now, TD, those types of communities, those people are waking up in the morning and after work going to rugby. So it’s cultural there, but rugby was always established there even in the bad old days of apartheid. It was there, it had germinated and it had spread and it was part of the culture. That’s still very much alive there, you look at the registration of clubs in the Eastern Cape I think fully 80 or 90% are “black clubs”. So that’s there, we’ve got challenges in other parts of the country. Yes, in the Western Cape and the Southern Cape, there are challenges there because you have a lot of old traditional coloured communities who have played rugby. So you have white communities, coloured communities, and to an extra extent black communities. There’s always going to be the cultural challenge, at the end of the day we want as many of our youngsters playing rugby.
Smith: Yea the cultural thing is interesting then, because it’s not necessarily a black white cultural thing. The University of Durban Westville here who tried to run a woman’s rugby team, they would look out – going back a couple of years because they didn’t field a team this year – they would look out for some girls coming from Eastern Cape to study because they know that somewhere along the line these girls have had contact with the game of rugby. So there was more chance of persuading them to come and play rugby than the Zulu girls arriving. The one thing that I would like to say that is underestimated by everyone is that unfortunately the game of rugby compared to many other sports is a complicated game. So it’s not a cultural or colour issue at all, if you went to an island of people, let’s say there were 5000 people living on this island who didn’t play one sport, rugby would probably be the last sport that you introduced to them because it’s just so complex. When the ball goes out to touch in soccer what do you do? You throw it in. In hockey you hit it in, in tennis it’s easy. So if you went to this island you would introduce rugby last, there’s so many rules involved.
HSSM: It’s kind of ironic because rugby has always been seen as a sport that’s played by not very smart people.
MacKenzie: Exactly.

“a lot of them come out of school and they’re too small, and it’s not their fault, it’s just the way it is.”

Smith: Yes, and it’s one of the hindrances of it. We’ve had Sharks doing development clinics out here in years gone by and as soon as they’ve turned around and walked to the change room the kid will get out a soccer ball and kick it to each other, and you spent an hour [laughs] teaching him to pass. Because it is easier, what are anyone’s kids going to do with their feet? They’ll sooner play a bit of soccer because it’s easier, you put a shoe there, you put a shoe there and you go. So the spread of rugby to non-rugby people, whether you’re talking about China, America – they are surprised in America, I spent time there in 1982, at how slowly… They convinced me that in ten years’ time (this was in 1982 when I coached rugby in America), they told me in ten years’ time they’d win the world cup because rugby was catching on there. [laughs] It’s still trying to catch on there because it’s a complicated game. When someone drops a ball you have a scrum, eight people go up against eight people and you gotta bind like this just to get the game going, so the complexity of it hinders our development of rugby when we’re introducing it to people without that rugby culture.
HSSM: Does this also play back to development, because of malnutrition the brain works slower and while they may be physically strong the brain might not grasp it due to nutrition?
Smith: No.
MacKenzie: I don’t think there’s a mental aspect, I’m not aware of that at all. But certainly from a physical point of view we’ve recognized that a lot of these youngsters, either when they leave school or during school, it’s a problem for them to compete. And it’s been recognized, South African Rugby have recognized it, in fact they’ve put in a nutrition program around the country now where there are high performance series to try and identify these boys and get them onto some eating program. Because a lot of them come out of school and they’re too small, and it’s not their fault, it’s just the way it is.

“if the white people don’t look at developing mass of black people then rugby has got very little future in this country”

Smith: The other frustrating thing, and this one of the important things I told you if you’re visiting, why have we not developed the game of rugby quicker than we have? The complicated game is one of the reasons, and the reason is that people, when they look and decide that we haven’t developed rugby as quickly as we should have in this country, what they’ll do is they’ll look at the elite, at the top end. That’s where they’ll look and they’ll make their decision, and they haven’t realized what a bottleneck this is. It’s a huge, huge bottleneck. To run out with a Springbok rugby jersey, thousands of white kids have failed, let alone black kids, on their way because it’s a huge bottleneck. To represent the Sharks it’s difficult, and I don’t think you’ll sit here and realize yourself just what a bottleneck there is in terms of getting representation and being on TV. No one’s going to comment too much on the fact that Rovers five years ago had virtually an all-white team, and has now got eight, nine, ten… No one’s worrying about that, they’re looking at this bottleneck at the top and they’re not considering the fact that thousands of white kids didn’t make it either. That’s one thing that sometimes frustrates me in terms of the judgement calls that people are making, too much is made of this.

There should be more, I think, in a country like ours. A guy like Willie Basson who’s been involved in rugby, if the white people don’t look at developing mass of black people then rugby has got very little future in this country because the white population is getting smaller. So rugby lovers like us have got to be conscious of the fact that hey, if we want to stay strong at Sharks level or at Springboks level, look at that elite side. Then we’ve got this mass of people to come and beat New Zealand in ten years’ time. They don’t have the forty something million people that we have. Australia doesn’t have that sort of numbers. We’ve got the numbers here. There are very few, even conservative white Afrikaners, who would be too stupid to see that. In terms of future we have to rely on the so called non-white population to remain strong.
MacKenzie: Ja, there’s no doubt we’ve got to utilize those numbers.

“this is a non-colour issue. It’s because of the bottleneck that they don’t come through.”

Smith: Another way of illustrating that bottleneck thing, because it’s not just political people who want to hone in on that bottleneck, we have headmasters of our top white rugby playing schools who will say something like, ‘oh you guys don’t look after the schoolboys. What happened to that guy? He played Natal Schools fullback then and that guy who played Natal Schools…’ You know what happened in fact? Andre Joubert keeps out eight years of Natal Schools fullbacks. Understand what I’m saying? Mark Andrews keeps out ten years of KZN schools locks because of that bottleneck. So when the people say oh you don’t look out for – Our players aren’t coming through! Now this is a non-colour issue. It’s because of the bottleneck that they don’t come through. There are just people better than them. I mean John Smit, Stefan Terblanche, these guys kept out fullbacks, they kept out props. It’s because you’re talking about very few people who can play there – very, very few people that can play there.
