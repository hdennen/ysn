Is High School Rugby Going Pro? KZNRU Leaders Interview Part 4

By: 
Sazi Luke
Harry Dennen
Mon, 12/11/2012 - 18:33

In part four of our KZNRU Leaders interview, Graham MacKenzie and Pete Smith speak to HSSM's Sazi Luke about KZN's tertiary problem, the future of rugby development, the professionalization of high school rugby, and what it takes to be a professional today.

(part 1, part 2, part 3)

"2% of our players, school leaders, are going on to play club rugby or stay on once the class is clear." -Graham MacKenzie

The KZN Tertiary Problem

HSSM: We have plenty of talent in the schools, why are so many of our players leaving? One of the topics that Jan [Jan van Straaten, Port Natal coach and KZNRU board member] raised is that we don’t have good enough tertiaries to keep these players.
MacKenzie: That’s a very good point.
Smith: Yes, a very good point.
HSSM: The only way for them to play rugby is with the Sharks Academy and not everyone can make it to the Sharks Academy, and without the Sharks Academy, the Sharks wouldn’t be where they are.
Smith: The Sharks Academy is filling that gap of a Tukkies or a Stellenbosch unfortunately.
MacKenzie: But it doesn’t fill it completely.
Smith: It’s just not big enough.

“our tertiary education lags behind the others around the country rugby-wise”

MacKenzie: The tertiary issue is a massive question and perceptions are that our tertiary education lags behind the others around the country rugby-wise.
HSSM: Why is that the reason? KZN rugby is rich with history, why aren’t the tertiary educations getting you on board?
MacKenzie: I would like you to go and have an interview [laughs] with Durban and I’d like you to give us the answer because there you have something like 25,000 students and we can only put together one side I think. So it’s crazy, I mean we should be having very strong tertiary rugby here at UKZN, DUT, Maritzburg University. The university does not support rugby and that’s the truth.
Smith: How seriously does that body regard rugby? That’s the question. Stellenbosch University, rugby is important. Funnily enough UCT rugby is important.
MacKenzie: If you look at the Varsity Cup, they take their rugby seriously. UWC, they’re taking it seriously now.
Smith: Tradition, culture, whatever you’d like to call it. You’re talking to two University of Natal ex-rugby players; we’re talking about our Alma Mater.

“This is the second biggest metro in the country and we don’t even have a Varsity Cup side.”

HSSM: I’ve just noticed we don’t have a team in the Varsity Cup. We have a team in the Varsity Shield, but not the Varsity Cup.
MacKenzie: It’s not acceptable. This is the second biggest metro in the country and we don’t even have a Varsity Cup side. How can that be? It’s impossible. Zululand is capable of having one. They’re capable of having at least a Shield. What I’m saying to you is that, in my mind, we should have about four in this province, Shield / Varsity Cup sides. That’s my thing, if we could get these tertiary institutions [on board].
HSSM: Has there been an attempt from the rugby union to work with the tertiary institutions?
MacKenzie: Ja, most definitely.
Smith: There was a meeting here two weeks ago […] with the senior people on the UW campus, which is the headquarters. Before that was two years ago, with Rudolph Straeuli and myself trying to persuade the people. Trevor was there saying that what happens with the Varsity Cup and the Varsity Shield is that a lot of publicity can be gained for these institutions. But it’s quite difficult, if there’s a school that’s just not traditionally a rugby school, one could take Northwood these days, they’re just not taking rugby seriously enough as they did in the days of UB 7’s and that. So it’s quite difficult to infiltrate that as a limited body with no finance. All you have to do is go and sit in the staff room there and psyche them up somehow. Imagine trying to get into the University of Natal headquarters here, it’s easy to psyche the rugby people up but it’s not so easy to encourage the administrators.
HSSM: Another point schools could make is that in Sharks Academy there’s not a lot of players that are originally from Natal, we are outsourcing from the Eastern Cape. What is happening to all the Natal players?
Smith: They’re welcome to go to the training.
HSSM: But what is the reason for them not going there?

“I’ve been frustrated about those two twins leaving to go to the Bulls”

MacKenzie: I think we have to be bluntly honest here. I think we are proponents of keeping local players here as much as possible and I’ve been frustrated about those two twins leaving to go to the Bulls. And I think there are other issues called money.
HSSM: Is that legal, them being approached at sixteen? Because they were approached at sixteen on a pre-contract.
MacKenzie: Well, that’s questionable; I don’t want to go there right now. Let’s look at the bigger picture. Every schoolboy in this province is entitled to apply to go to the academy, and I would venture to say that our province doesn’t have the same depth of talent in rugby at schools as other provinces do. You just have to look at the results of Craven Week. When did KZN last win or be ranked the number one side at Craven Week? You gotta go back a hell of a long time to find that out. Now are we thereabouts, do we win two out of three? Yes. Do we ever win the big prize? No. So we have enough to put together a very competitive side, but the Bulls for example have three decent teams in Pretoria alone. Western Province have five in Cape Town right under their noses. In Paarl alone they have three teams, Stellenbosch another three teams. So in a radius of 50 kilometres they’ve got massive schools. Then you just go to another corridor and you’ve got the Rondebosches and the SACS – we don’t have that here. You’ve got probably six top schools who play rugby. Six, that’s it. So people think Natal rugby is that strong, I’m not so sure that that’s correct compared to other provinces. Like this year, we had five really good players on our Craven Week side and we need to keep all five and we haven’t for whatever reason. I don’t want to speculate on that now but it’s… We need to keep those five. But the other guys who weren’t at that level are still welcome to come here.

“We will contract ten, twelve juniors.”

HSSM: So how do the Sharks, which is the mother body of all these teams, manage to win things like the Currie Cup? Is it outsourcing their players?
MacKenzie: No, they just buy astutely. That’s what you gotta do. You have a nursery coming though which is your academy, having your under 19s and under 21s, hopefully some of those will contribute, and the rest you buy from the outside.
Smith: That’s a professional game.
MacKenzie: That’s what every franchise does.
Smith: Those years have gone by where the Sharks, hopefully they’re from here. You look at soccer in Europe. […] They’re in the professional area, it’s rands and cents, it’s not where the guy’s from. We’ve got a different approach to the Blue Bulls, for example. They will recruit, with assistance from Tukkies University, youngsters and contract thirty something. We will contract ten, twelve juniors.

Where Is KZNRU Heading?

HSSM: Where is it from here in terms of KZN Rugby Union? What is the plan for the next ten years from now in terms of developing the schools, developing underprivileged background kids, developing rugby at tertiary level? What is the plan forward?

“It’s got to be sustainable, go from a strong base and slowly expand the game into areas where we haven’t played”

MacKenzie: […] I would venture to say that our development program is superior to what football’s is. Football, because it’s played 95% by historically disadvantaged guys and mainly black guys as opposed to the other minority groups, they think they’ve got it good for transformation, but that’s not transformation. They’re not developing the game in other areas at all. I’m not here to criticize them, but just to say that because of the natural breakdown of our population they get a lot less flak than we do, and that’s reality in terms of development. But you asked the question, ‘where do we go from here?,’ in February we had an indaba which was held down in the South Coast. We transformed our union, we’ve amended our constitution in some areas and I believe we are now a fully-fledged and integrated union with no biasness of the past to allow, at the executive level, historically disadvantaged people to participate equally and they’re not disadvantaged in any way, so we’ve done that. The mother body has cleaned up its act and we’ve gone on to the 21st century. The second thing we decided down there is that we had to grow the game, and grow the game means going to schools, clubs, townships, and so on. Pete touched on the fact that one of our hugest challenges here are funds to grow the game. The government aren’t going to help us, we need to go to corporate South Africa to try and help us. When I say government not helping us, they do, we would like more help from lotteries, from government, all of those departments that can. But in order to grow the vision, to get more schools playing rugby, we want to get in each region another two schools a year playing if we can. There’s no use getting another hundred playing, because it’ll fall down. It’s got to be sustainable, go from a strong base and slowly expand the game into areas where we haven’t played. That is our mission statement, but we are under financed right now. That doesn’t mean to say we’re going to stop, that is our mission statement.

“everybody is fighting over a shrinking base of talented players”

The Semi-Professional Nature of High School Rugby

HSSM: What is your feeling on school rugby becoming semi-professional? There’s been a lot of money being pumped into it and it’s becoming extremely competitive, especially at that age. And where it’s not about the fun and love of the sport, there’s a lot of reasons behind winning and losing now. What is your take on that?
MacKenzie: Well firstly I’d like to say rugby has always, at school level, been highly competitive. But what’s happened in the last ten years, I’d venture to say, is that the intake of players as the talent pool has shrunk and more schools have said we can get more notoriety from being a top class rugby school – because rugby is a big, big thing on campus – everybody is fighting over a shrinking base of talented players and it’s just that there aren’t enough. So when you have a scarce league of players, good players, people start making stupid and absurd offers to these guys. It used to be a 20% bursary, then it was fifty, then it became 100% and now in some cases it’s 120% up to 150%. Now I’m not a mathematician, but how the hell do you get to 150? That is what’s happening on the ground.
Smith: [laughs] That’s pocket money, the other fifty.

“Now it’s business, it’s a career, it’s a way for them to gain notoriety”

MacKenzie: Ja, so it’s becoming a little bit silly, but it’s part of the professional era and let’s face it, a parent today, he might have allegiances to DHS but if someone else come to him and says hey, you’re gonna get 100% schooling just come to this school, he’s not going to turn it down especially if he needs… most families need a bit of help. It’s a tough deal right now. But it’s life, it happens in the States, we followed it. It’s happening in the UK, everybody’s scrambling for the top players and that’s the professional era we’re in. boys now see rugby as a career, not just as a thing you went and played club rugby. Now it’s business, it’s a career, it’s a way for them to gain notoriety, whether it’s in sports management, refereeing –there’s a whole series of options open so that’s why people are getting into the game.
Smith: Yea, it’s unfortunate and for many people it’s distasteful, but one’s just got to understand it’s a reality and we, perhaps being involved in the sport professional rugby, might accept it, be grudgingly. But there are a lot of people out there, one of the headmasters at a school recently, he just used the word ‘disgraceful’. That might be the case for many watching what’s going on in the top schools because it doesn’t seem right that a sixteen or seventeen year old is commanding fees. It just doesn’t seem right.

“they’re going to go where the biggest dollar is”

HSSM: But doesn’t that increase the quality of rugby? Doesn’t it, at the end of the day, make rugby at Sharks a bit better because it’s so competitive at high school level?
MacKenzie: But what happens if they leave and go to the Bulls, like we’ve just seen? It’s the professional era. Those guys, they’re going to go where the biggest dollar is. The two Kriel brothers, who I have known personally, in fact I got them to Maritzburg College […] many years ago. So my question is: do you think that the Kriels have gone to the Bulls because they like their colours? Or because they think the coaching is better, that they’ve got a better upward career? Could it be financial or could it be a little bit of all those things? And it’s probably a little bit of each, but at the end of the day they’re young professionals now. They’ve signed on with a professional franchise, and they’re going to go where they think they have the best chance to develop and have big careers.
HSSM: Are we doing something to prevent other players from going, like the Du Preez brothers who are only in grade eleven and they’re on the SA Schools team. Are we doing something not to lose them? I know their father played for the Blue Bulls so they might go.
MacKenzie: He also played for this province.
Smith: He moved down here, ja.
MacKenzie: He coaches Rovers now.
HSSM: So are we doing something not to lose those boys?
MacKenzie: I would like to think that we are. [laughs] We’ll leave it at that.
Smith: It’ll be a financial thing in the end. […] We’ve got a situation where Western Province, with all their talented schools, they’ve got two very good flyhalfs at school level. The one is going to the Bulls and the one is coming here.

What It Takes To Be A Professional Today

“they’ve got to work extremely hard, they’ve got to be diligent, they can’t have their heads turned by the glamour of lights because if they do they’re not going to make it today.”

HSSM: What message would both of you gentlemen have for young kids who are aspiring to be professional rugby players at high school? They played first team and they want to know what it takes to be a professional rugby player. Because that’s everyone’s dream who plays rugby, it’s not the reality for all of them because only, as you said, 1% make it, but what do they have to do, what message would you have for them to make it there?
Smith: To become a professional?
HSSM: To play for the Sharks, to wear the Sharks colours one day.
Smith: You saw that bottleneck right there – work blooming hard. [laughs]
MacKenzie: I think number one is they’ve got to work extremely hard, they’ve got to be diligent, they can’t have their heads turned by the glamour of lights because if they do they’re not going to make it today. Because someone else will take your slot, there’s someone just as good who will be more disciplined. I think they’ve got to be absolutely committed to the cause, they have to have good luck and not get injuries and they mustn’t fall into a potential bottleneck that Peter said. So hopefully, if they’ve done their homework and they’ve looked at who’s in the Sharks and who’s there at lock, maybe it’s a lock and the current lock is 29, they would say well that’s a good deal.

“Anton Bresler’s worked bloody hard and he’s done it on sheer grit and dedication, so it can be done.”

But if the guy’s 21 and he’s 18, those are the type of things to look at to avoid potential bottlenecks. But you’ve got to stay fit, you’ve got to be perfectly dedicated, and you’ve got to know that someone else is going to work very, very hard. Anton Bresler is the greatest example: goes to DHS and he wasn’t picked up here by this academy. He went to Zululand, came back, played for Rovers and then went to this academy after he had a good under 20 season. He’s worked bloody hard and he’s done it on sheer grit and dedication, so it can be done. It depends how you live your life if that’s what you want to do. Φ
